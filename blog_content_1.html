Can I afford a $400k house on $80k?

Understanding what house price you can afford involves more than just your income. While an $80,000 annual salary is a solid income, a $400,000 house is a significant investment that requires careful consideration of several financial factors.

The 28/36 Rule Revisited

Lenders often use the 28/36 rule as a guideline. This suggests that your monthly housing costs (mortgage principal and interest, property taxes, and homeowner's insurance - PITI) should not exceed 28% of your gross monthly income, and your total debt-to-income (DTI) ratio (including housing costs and all other monthly debt payments) should not exceed 36%.

  • **Gross Monthly Income**: $80,000 / 12 = $6,666.67
  • **28% Housing Limit**: $6,666.67 * 0.28 = $1,866.67

This means your total monthly housing payment should ideally be around $1,866.67 or less. A $400,000 mortgage, even with a good interest rate, will likely exceed this amount, especially when factoring in property taxes and insurance.

Key Factors Beyond Income:

1. **Down Payment**: A larger down payment reduces the amount you need to borrow, lowering your monthly mortgage payments. For a $400,000 house, a 20% down payment is $80,000. If you put down less, you might also have to pay Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI).

2. **Interest Rate**: Even a small difference in interest rates can significantly impact your monthly payment and the total cost of the loan. Your credit score plays a crucial role in securing the best rates.

3. **Property Taxes and Homeowner's Insurance**: These vary significantly by location and can add hundreds of dollars to your monthly housing costs. Don't forget to factor these in!

4. **Other Debts**: Car loans, student loans, credit card debt â€“ these all contribute to your DTI ratio. If your other monthly debt payments are high, it will reduce the amount of house you can afford.

5. **Closing Costs**: These are fees associated with buying a home and can range from 2% to 5% of the loan amount. For a $400,000 house, this could be $8,000 to $20,000, which needs to be paid upfront.

Example Scenario:

Let's assume a $400,000 house with a $40,000 down payment (10%), a 30-year fixed mortgage at 7% interest, and estimated property taxes/insurance of $400/month. Your principal and interest payment alone would be around $2,394. Add in taxes and insurance, and your total monthly housing cost could be close to $2,794. This is significantly higher than the $1,866.67 suggested by the 28% rule for an $80,000 salary.

Conclusion:

While an $80,000 salary might make a $400,000 house challenging to afford comfortably, it's not impossible. It would likely require a substantial down payment, a very low-interest rate, minimal other debts, and a willingness to stretch your budget beyond the typical guidelines.
